Introduction to Ethics

In contemporary society, the word “ethics” tends to conjure up a cornucopia of negative connotations.  We usually encounter the term when it is woven into tales of scandal, liability, prosecution, political fallout, and corporate corruption.  The word “morality” is perhaps equally unappealing; in the minds of many, it mainly signifies an obligatory repression of sensuality.  In terms of Epicureanism, however, “ethics” and “morality” are synonymous terms that simply mean: strategy.  Ethical or moral issues do not center upon legalese and lust, but rather upon selecting an ultimate goal (or telos in Greek) and practicing a consistent policy of choice and avoidance to achieve that goal.

But before we attempt to set forth, we must first settle an important preliminary matter: is it even possible for an individual to shape the course of his life in the first place?  Or is the future predestined by inexorable circumstances beyond his control?  To assert the former is to take the position of free will; to assume the latter is to take the position of fatalism.

Free Will and its Detractors

Epicureanism calls for a firm belief in the existence of free will, i.e. volition, without which its ethical teachings would have little meaning.  For what’s the use of studying a system of choice and avoidance if all of our choices are predetermined by fate in the first place?

This issue has been sharply debated by philosophers and scientists alike throughout recorded history.  Perhaps the most prevalent challenge to the position of free will is the physical doctrine of determinism.  While this view of the universe is a convenient for mathematicians, it also carries the baggage of some strange portents.  For if the universe was indeed deterministic, then everything that ever happens—from the far reaches of outer-space to the inner-sanctums of every human brain—would have to occur out of necessity.  Life would be entirely non-interactive: “every breath you take and every move you make” would be choreographed for all eternity, with no chance whatsoever to deviate even slightly from nature’s script.  This scenario is hardly compatible with the idea that we have the power to sway the outcome of our lives!  But while most adherents of determinism do reject volition, other determinists are certainly uncomfortable in doing so, judging by the number of desperate proposals to reconcile the two beliefs!  Arguments for this sort of “compatibilism” are as wide-ranging in approach as they are numerous—but they all tend to focus on semantics rather than the substance of the dilemma.  Epicurus, however, circumvents this paradox by calmly rejecting determinism outright.

A more serious challenge to the position of free will is the argument of reductionism.  Whether the universe is deterministic or indeterministic is of no consequence to the reductionist’s argument—he will assert that free will cannot exist in either scenario.  For if we accept the materialist premise that the mind must be an ensemble of matter, then to assert that the mind has the power of choice is tantamount to ascribing this power to “matter itself.”  But if we also accept the premise of non-teleology—that bits of matter are passive agents bungling through space and time without purpose nor intention—then how can we suppose that a purposeful entity can a purposeless universe?  “So what if matter has some unpredictable attributes,” the reductionist shrugs, “all this implies for the mind is that it is a puppet of random forces rather than fixed ones.”  In neither case, he concludes, can matter be directed by deliberate conscious control.

It is true enough that while the position of the indeterminism, such as Epicurus' doctrine of the atomic swerve, allows for the possibility for voluntary action, indeterminism by itself does not guarantee its existence.  A human statue, after all, might contain about as many atoms as you do—and while the atoms in marble may “swerve” just as much as the atoms in your flesh, it doesn’t therefore follow that a statue has free will!  Obviously, there must be an important difference between statues and humans, and there is: the way they are built.  Volition is a secondary attribute, which depends just as much upon the atomic configuration of its constituent matter as it does upon the very presence of the matter itself.  It is possible for an active will to emerge from passive atoms in the same way that life, consciousness, and intelligence can emerge from atoms which are lifeless, unconscious, and utterly oblivious.  This is why the reductionist, who looks for volition in one atom at a time, will indeed never find it.  What he neglects to understand is the principle of synergy—i.e., the properties of the whole are not always limited to the properties of its part.  For if they were, then there could not even be any such thing as awareness.  Your mind would not really exist—there would just be a human body that, while it lasts, responds to your name and exhibits “complex behavior.”

Introspective evidence tells us otherwise.  We do appreciate the spectacular difference between awareness and oblivion.  We experience the reality of life.  We feel pleasure and pain.  Likewise, we can discern the difference between what it is like to be compelled and what it is like to initiate action.  The distinction is as self-evident today as it was over two thousand years ago when Lucretius observed:

We change direction not because it is time to do so
or because we are where we must, but because we want to.
Without a doubt, it is our wills that begin these movements
which are carried out through our limbs.
Haven’t you seen, at the moment the barriers open
at the start of a race, the horses, as if hesitating,
unable to throw themselves forward as fast as they want to?
The whole of their matter has to be brought into motion,
which means that the messages have to run through their bodies
till every bit is altered and moves with the mind.
As you see, the impetus comes from within;
The movement starts in the mind and in the will;
From there it spreads through the limbs and through the whole body.

It is not at all the same thing when we move at the instance
of an overpowering force, or simply because someone pushes us.
In that case the material of the whole body
obviously moves in spite of us, hurried onwards
until the will succeeds in curbing it.
The fact is that external forces may move us
and hurry us onwards by the scruff of our necks;
Yet in spite of this, there still is something inside us
which can put up a struggle and get in the way of them.
It is this which controls the material of our bodies
and by a certain adjustment of our limbs
brings them up in their flight and returns them to rest.
Lucretius, On Nature II.259

So having recognized our capacity for volition as genuine, we may now review Epicurus’ strategic guidelines with the necessary confidence in our ability to make use of it.

The Telos of Epicurean Ethics

Epicurus identified ataraxia—the absence of disturbance—as the pinnacle of pleasure and the ideal moral goal.  He did not choose this goal arbitrarily; he did so because of its special significance in human nature.  He observed that humans, like all other sentient creatures which inhabit the earth, are naturally constituted to relish pleasure, and abhor pain.  Hence, we are already predisposed to seek out the former, and to avoid the latter.  When at last we find ourselves undisturbed by any sort of discomfort whatsoever, we feel fully satisfied and lack the urge to seek out anything more.  At this point, one has arrived, Epicurus surmises, at “the goal prescribed by nature.”  Lucius Torquatus, the Epicurean proponent featured in Cicero’s De Finibus (“On Goals”), puts this matter in clear perspective: 

The truth of the proposition that pleasure is the ultimate good will most readily appear from the following illustration.  Let us imagine a man living in the continuous enjoyment of numerous and vivid pleasures alike of body and of mind, undisturbed either by the presence or by the prospect of pain: what possible state of existence could we describe as being more excellent or more desirable? … His lot will be one which will not admit of further improvement.  Suppose, on the other hand, a person crushed beneath the heaviest load of mental and of bodily anguish to which humanity is liable.  Grant him no hope of ultimate relief in view; also give him no pleasure either present or in prospect.  Can one describe or imagine a more pitiable state?

If then, a life full of pain is the thing most to be avoided, it follows that to live in pain is the highest evil; and this position implies that a life of pleasure is the ultimate good. … This being so, it clearly follows that actions are right and praiseworthy only as being a means to the attainment of a life of pleasure.  But that which is not itself a means to anything else, but to which all else is a means, is what the Greeks term the telos, the highest, ultimate or final Good.  It must therefore be admitted that the chief Good is to live happily. - Cicero, On Ends, Good and Bad I.XII

This is the appealing destiny that Epicurus beckons us to fulfill.    What is now left to us is the task of finding our way there.

Enlightened Hedonism

Because Epicurus integrates pleasure into the ideal moral goal, Epicureanism can be rightly regarded as a morality of hedonism.  We have already made the observation that we are predisposed by nature to pursue pleasure—so what more is there to know?   Plenty.   To suppose that “pursuing pleasure” is all one needs to know about living the ethics of hedonism is like supposing that “following the road” is all there is to know about driving a car; some additional knowledge and skill are required to negotiate freeway interchanges and hairpin turns—none is required to get lost, or to plow into embankments.  Hence, Epicureans aren’t the kind of impulsive, cavalier “hedonists” who are commonly associated with the term.  Epicureanism entails a more deliberate approach to pursuing pleasures—one in which the potential for negative consequences does not escape our consideration.

“No pleasure is a bad thing in itself,” Epicurus reminds us in his eighth Principal Doctrine, “but the means which produce some pleasures bring with them disturbances many times greater than the pleasures themselves.”  Hence, not all pleasurable avenues are deemed choice-worthy.  If, for example, someone elects to drink himself into a stupor in the evening, only to wake up with head-splitting hangover the following day, then he is not a very successful hedonist by Epicurean standards!  The pleasure he gained to begin with was offset by the discomfort which came afterwards.  Because many activities can bring mixed results in terms of pleasure and pain, it is essential to factor in both sides of the scale, so to speak, to see which way the balance is tipped—otherwise, we risk losing ground in our journey towards our intended goal. Progress cannot be made by taking one step forward and two steps back.  This is why alcoholics are typically unhappy: it’s not because being inebriated is unpleasurable, but because drinking excessively brings unsettling consequences in its wake.

Hence, Epicureans realize that any method of achieving short-term comfort which necessitates a long-term annoyance should be avoided.  While on the other hand, any long-term comfort that can be secured at the price of a brief annoyance is a hedonistic bargain worthy of selection.  Ancient sources reveal that Epicurus had set forth some practical guidelines in a book entitled “On Choice and Avoidance,” but the manuscript itself has since been lost to us.  Fortunately, we do have enough in the way of commentary and other writings to enable us to piece together his advice.

The 3-Fold Classifications of Desires

One surviving maxim in particular, the twenty-ninth Principal Doctrine, serves as the best starting point—Epicurus’ three-fold classification of desires.  “Among desires,” he wrote:

[1] some are natural and necessary,
[2] some natural but not necessary,
[3] and others neither natural nor necessary, but due to vain opinion.
- Epicurus, Principal Doctrine 29

“Natural” in this context, means abundant in nature, while “necessary” means necessary to remove pain.  These distinctions will become clearer as we proceed through some examples.

The class of “natural and necessary” desires are for those things will necessarily lead to greater pain if they are not fulfilled—yet, in ordinary circumstances, they can be fulfilled rather easily.  Nourishment, shelter, health, companionship, and a sense of security are interrelated requirements which all belong to this basic category.

The class of “natural and unnecessary” desires are for those things which do not necessarily lead to greater pain if left unfulfilled, even though fulfillment may be easy to obtain.  These appetites are typically recreational in nature: theatre, travel, cuisine, sexual intercourse, and so on.

The class of “unnatural and unnecessary” desires are for those things which not only do not necessarily lead to greater pain if they are not fulfilled, but are realized only at the price of an ongoing burden.  Fame, status, political power, extraordinary wealth, and other ambitions which have the trappings of “prestige” all belong to this last category.

Epicurean Strategies

In dealing with each class of desires, Epicurus recommends particular strategies.  First of all, we should go about satisfying the necessary desires in the most economical way possible.  A predominately simple and nutritious diet will satisfy hunger and health, modest living quarters can adequately supply physical comfort, and consummating friendships will go far to secure both the need for companionship and confidence of aid in times of distress.  Studying the nature of the universe, so that we may confidently reject the absurdities of superstition, is also essential to enhancing our sense of security.

Secondly, our efficiency in dealing with the preceding frees up our remaining resources to explore the large variety of “natural and unnecessary” desires.  These we may pursue to our heart’s content, so long as they do not interfere with our ability to satisfy our natural and necessary desires.  For example, we must never risk nor sacrifice our health, friendships, finances, or standing with the law, for the sake of pursuing leisure.  If we ever feel tempted to do so, it’s best to switch our attention to a less costly desire in this abundant category so that we may receive our pleasures untarnished by the prospects of future hardship.

Thirdly, we come to the “unnatural and unnecessary” desires, for which Epicurus’ advice is unequivocal: we should avoid them entirely.  This is because the fulfillment of unnatural desires is difficult to attain, and impossible to maintain.  We may, for instance, relish the limelight of fame (which seems to last up to “fifteen minutes” according to twentieth-century artist Andy Warhol!) but we may have to put up with stalkers and the paparazzi for a lifetime.  Political power invariably creates enemies, and often attracts hostility, usurpers, or even assassins.  Opulent wealth attracts thieves, kidnappers, and politicians. It’s no wonder that Classical Epicureans lived by the motto “Live in anonymity!”  For ultimately, the pinnacle of ataraxia can be the same experience for everybody—be they eminent or unknown, rich or poor, young or old, sophisticated or simple.

Hence, the Epicurean keeps the objective of experiential bliss always close at hand, merely by keeping himself well-nourished, well-housed, well-educated, and well-entertained.  These basic requirements can be satisfied with a modest income, but they are by no means any less rewarding than the wider range of more competitive undertakings commonly held to be prerequisites for emotional fulfillment.  For just as it’s usually more feasible to adjust our expenses than our income, it is usually more feasible still to adjust our attitudes.  “If you wish to make Pythocles rich,” Epicurus once counseled, “do not give him more money, but diminish his desire.”  For happiness is an internal victory that transcends outward circumstances.  Getting the most out of life is therefore not a matter of somehow “outdoing” the victories of others, but rather a matter of prolonging victories within ourselves.

Enlightened Egoism

This emphasis on self-awareness makes Epicurean ethics egoistic—a term which is no less often abused than hedonistic.  But just as it is possible to conceive of a hedonist whose concerns extend beyond the pleasures of the immediate moment, so too is it possible to conceive of an egoist whose self-interest extends beyond the horizon of his epidermis.  For as long as human beings continue to live in a social context, it will always be to our mutual advantage to appreciate each other’s concerns, so that we might effectively collaborate with one another in our common journey towards achieving ataraxia.  This distinctive custom of goodwill is best exemplified by Epicurus’ own illustrious life.  His biographer, Diogenes Laërtius, reports that…

There are plenty of witnesses of the unsurpassable kindness of the man to everybody—both his own country which honored him with brazen statues, and his friends who are so numerous that they could not be counted in whole cities. - Diogenes Laërtius, Lives X.V

It is not by accident that Epicurus acquired his venerable reputation; he practiced kindness intentionally, and consequently procured a tremendous pleasure-payoff in the form of reciprocated kindness from the multitudes of admirers who befriended him.  Consequently, a deep reverence for the institution of friendship is a renowned hallmark of Epicureanism.  In De Finibus, we see this attitude reflected in the monologue of Torquatus, where he explains to Cicero why friendship is a hedonistic imperative:

Your school [Cicero’s Stoicism] maintains that if pleasure be the Chief Good, friendship will cease to exist.  Now Epicurus’ pronouncement about friendship is that of all the means to happiness that wisdom has devised, none is greater, none more fruitful, none more delightful than this.  Nor did he only commend this doctrine by his eloquence, but far more by the example of his life and conduct.  How great a thing friendship is, is shown by the mythical stories of antiquity.  Review the legends from the remotest ages, and, copious and varied as they are, you will barely find in them three pairs of friends, beginning with Theseus and ending with Orestes.  Yet Epicurus in single house, and that a small one, maintained a whole company of friends, united by the closest sympathy and affection; and this still goes on in the Epicurean school.

[…]

A solitary, friendless life must be beset by secret dangers and alarms.  Hence reason itself advises the acquisition of friends; their possession gives confidence, and a firmly rooted hope of winning pleasure.  And just as hatred, jealousy and contempt are hindrances to pleasure, so friendship is the most trustworthy preserver and also creator of pleasure alike for our friends and for ourselves.  It affords us enjoyment in the present, and it inspires us with hopes for the near and distant future.  Thus it is not possible to secure uninterrupted gratification in life without friendship, nor yet to preserve friendship itself unless we love our friends as much as ourselves.  Hence this unselfishness does occur in friendship, while also friendship is closely linked with pleasure.  For we rejoice in our friends’ joy as much as in our own, and are equally painted by their sorrows. - Cicero, On Ends, Good and Bad I.XX

It is therefore only natural that the Epicurean ethic engenders magnanimity and empathy.  As far-sighted intelligent beings, it isn’t hard for us to realize what advantages there are in having friends to rely on in times of need, versus facing life’s challenges in isolation.

Cardinal Virtues

It is with similar consistency that Epicurus recommends acting in ways that are widely regarded as noble, decent, and righteous. These precepts are the cardinal virtues: wisdom, honor, and justice.  In the context of Epicureanism however, their significance is not rooted in peer-approval, but rather in their productiveness of pleasure within ourselves.  In his fifth Principal Doctrine, Epicurus states that the relationship between pleasure and virtue is inseparable: 

It is impossible to live a pleasant life without living wisely, honorably and justly, and it is impossible to live wisely, honorably and justly without living pleasantly.  Whenever any one of these is lacking, when, for instance, the man is not able to live wisely, though he lives honorably and justly, it is impossible for him to live a pleasant life. - Epicurus, Principal Doctrine 5

It is wisdom (also rendered as “intelligence” or “prudence” in translation) which Epicurus identifies as the most fundamental cardinal virtue.  To live wisely, by Epicurean standards, is to bring a broad awareness of Nature down to the level of our daily existence, thereby establishing a context for intelligent choice-making.  Epicurus held that the most common obstacles to happiness were, in fact, due to lapses in our understanding of Nature.  In bringing forth enlightenment, Epicurus formulated his tetrapharmakos, or “four-fold remedy.”  This set of four precepts is found among the opening maxims of Epicurus’ forty “Principal Doctrines,” and is summarized by Philodemus thus: “God presents no fears, death no worries.  And while good is readily attainable, evil is readily endurable.”

The second cardinal virtue named by Epicurus is honor.  To live honorably is to have the courage to persevere in the face of difficulties, whenever the prospects for ideal happiness become obstructed.  This implies that we assert the moral course we set for ourselves with consistency, without ever betraying our professed standards for the sake of expediency.  Honor also engenders fair treatment—a willingness to pay respect in accordance with how we ourselves would like to be respected, and conversely, an unwillingness to tolerate abuse.

Thirdly, Epicurus recognizes the virtue of justice.  Like the other virtues, justice is not considered by Epicureans to be an “absolute good,” but rather an obligatory means for securing happiness.  The nature of this obligation is contractual; justice is grounded upon a promise not to initiate harm against one’s peers, nor to permit such wrongdoing to go unpunished.  Throughout history, it has proved mutually advantageous for individuals to band together into societies implicitly or explicitly embracing this kind of pact, because, according to Epicurus, “the greatest fruit of justice is peace of mind.”  When we live under conditions of assured protection, we can derive solace from the expectation that we will not fall prey to criminal acts, or in the unlikely event that we do, that there will at least be avenues of recourse available to us.  On the other hand, those who try to cheat the social covenant by committing crimes are subject to perpetual anxieties that accompany the prospects of looming retaliation.

The rewards for adopting the cardinal virtues as a matter of personal policy are of no small consequence.  The difference between living wisely or unwisely makes the difference between a life of fulfillment versus a life of bewilderment.  The difference between living honorably or dishonorably makes the difference between a life galvanized by a sense of dignity versus a life undermined by a sense of shame.  The difference between living justly or unjustly makes the difference between harboring a sense of security versus a sense of futility.  Collectively, the degree to which these virtues are put into practice directly corresponds to the degree to which misery is expelled and happiness achieved.

Politics

Epicureanism may seem wholly apolitical, especially when compared to the extensive treatment of political matters given by his most prominent philosophical predecessors: Plato and Aristotle.  For Epicurus did not develop a particular schema for an ideal government—he was instead concerned about what constitutes the ideal individual.

But the ethics of Epicureanism is not without political implications.  “Laws,” says Epicurus, “are made for the sake of the wise, not to prevent them from inflicting wrong but to save them from suffering it.”  Hence, Epicureans are keen to determine whether their political climate is conducive to justice or if it falls short of this noble paradigm or perhaps even works against it.  In cases where disagreeable laws are in force, Epicurus still recommends obedience, so long as the sacrifices required are insignificant; but if the demands of society become excessive, it’s time to move on, as Epicurus himself found occasion to do so when he fled Mytilene (This was the locale of Epicurus’ first school.  At issue was the educational monopoly enforced by local officials, the “gymnasiarchs.”)  The Homeric character Teucer sums up the Epicurean political attitude succinctly with his line: “My country is wherever I am happy.”
