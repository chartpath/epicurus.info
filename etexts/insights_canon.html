Consciousness

As conscious organisms, we human beings do not merely react to the universe, but experience it.  Even though, according to atomism, an individual’s brain and body are ultimately comprised of solid impregnable elements, the structure as a whole forms an immensely complex physique that is delicate and pliable.  The focal point of experience is that which we regard as consciousness, otherwise known as the intellect, the mind, or the soul, which Epicurus described as being...

… composed of light particles, diffused over all the members of the body…  There exists in it a special part, endowed with an extreme mobility, in consequence of the exceeding slightness of the elements which compose it, and also in reference to its more immediate sympathy with the rest of the body. - Epicurus, Letter to Herodotus 63

But we must dispel with the religious connotations that the term “soul” conjures up.  The soul is neither immaterial nor immortal, but rather a distinctive component of a living organism.  That the soul is affected by the senses is proof enough of its material nature, for if the soul was utterly immaterial, then it would be no different from empty space; mass and energy would pass right through it, and the surrounding universe could not be detected by the mind in any way.  But in reality, the soul is continuously bombarded by mass and energy, yielding an evident perception of our environment.

The Passions

The range of what is knowable to the human intellect is not limited to what surrounds and impinges us; the inner self, beneath our skin, is a complex realm of intrigue that contributes profoundly to our repertoire of experiences.  No theory of knowledge would be complete without its inclusion.

The mentalities of all conscious beings are structured in such a manner that the sensations may have a direct influence on behavior: some sensations sustain the current state of mind, inclining an organism to maintain its present course of action; while others disrupt the state of mind, inclining an organism to change its course of action.  These two phenomena are respectively experienced as pleasure and pain, and collectively termed the passions in the parlance of classical philosophers.  What a living being shall experience as pleasurable and painful is not randomly assigned at birth—the passions are inherently biased by our nature.  For Epicurus in particular, pleasure and pain are of central importance to his system of ethics.

Presentations & Representations

In the terminology of Epicurean philosophy, any sensory event that can be consciously registered is regarded as a presentation.  Every time we receive a presentation, sensory impulses impinges our minds, giving us an instinctive ability to recognize reoccurring themes.  Similar shapes projected upon our eyes, similar sounds reverberating in our ears, similar tastes and smells that tickle our palate, similar feelings that touch us inside and out—all of these patterns are reinforced into our memories with each occurrence, giving rise to representations (i.e., preconceptions) in our imagination.

An ancient inscription on the wall of Oinoanda describes this phenomenon reasonably well:

What is viewed by sight is impressed upon the soul, and after the impression of the original images, passages are opened up in us in such a way that, even when the objects which we originally saw are no longer present, our mind bears representations of the original objects. 
- Diogenes of Oinoanda, new fragment 5.3.3-14

Attributes

Through the accumulation of representations we learn to recognize attributes: shapes, sizes, hues, tones, flavors, scents, textures, etc.  Epicurean philosophy posits that some attributes are axiomatic, the primary attributes, while the rest are the synergistic: secondary attributes.  Primary attributes are those innate qualities which are inseparable from the basic constitution of the universe: the solidity, size, shape, weight, and motion of the atoms—the intangibility, boundlessness, and (more recently conjectured) shape of the void.  Secondary attributes are founded upon the positional arrangements of multiple atoms that, by happenstance, produce presentations that are distinguishable by the mind.

For instance, the tangibility of a leaf is derived from a primary attribute: the solidity of the atoms within it.  But the greenness of a leaf is a secondary attribute—it is not comprised of “green atoms.”  Atoms themselves are colorless, but they can be regularly spaced in such a way that they give off a stream of photons at a particular energy level.  Because there are photoreceptors behind our retinas geared to absorb photons at a similar level, the photons are converted into distinctive neural impulses.  These are in turn registered by the mind, and we describe what we see as green.

Secondary attributes, real as they are, do not arise by necessity.  Large objects are not necessarily made out of large atoms, rectangular objects do not derive their shape from rectangular atoms, nor must a stationary object contain stationary atoms.  Even more obviously, striped objects are not painted with “striped atoms,” nor does the mind contain “intelligent atoms.”  The qualities of individual atoms are generic and few—but in combinations they produce innumerable effects.

Opinions

Having established the infallibility of the senses does not, however, put us on the path towards omniscience.  Sensory presentations are utterly reliable, but we must also recognize that they are not utterly available.  Nothing in the universe produces sights, sounds, or smells that are accessible everywhere to everyone at every time.  Objects of minute size or at remote distances may produce effects that are below our threshold of detection, rendering them invisible, silent, or odorless.  Even large objects nearby are regularly shielded from our eyes, ears, and noses by intervening barriers.  The only phenomenon in the universe which is omnipresent, the void, is intangible, thus producing no sensation of any kind.

Since we cannot broaden the range of our observations to penetrate every nook and cranny of the universe, we must make do with sweeping assessments.  By this means, we form opinions.  Unlike the staunch presentations, which are always true, opinions are a matter of contrivance—their veracity is contingent upon the ongoing testimony of sensory presentations.  This is the crux of Epicurus’ Canon, and Sextus Empiricus extends us the courtesy of spelling out its articles:

According to Epicurus, some opinions are true, some false.  True opinions are those which are attested by and not contested by clear facts, while false opinions are those which are contested by and not attested by clear facts.

Attestation is perception through a self-evident impression, that the object of opinion is such as it once was thought to be—for  example, if Plato is approaching from far off, I form the conjectural opinion, owing to the distance, that he is Plato.  But when he has come close, there is further testimony that he is Plato, now that the distance is reduced, and it is attested by the self-evidence itself.

Non-contestation is the conformity between a non-evident thing which is the object of speculation, and the opinion about what is apparent—for example, Epicurus, in saying that void exists, which is non-evident, confirms this through the self-evident fact of motion.  For if void does not exist, there ought not be motion either, since the moving body would lack a place to pass into as a consequence of everything being full and solid.  Therefore, the non-evident thing believed is not contradicted by that which is evident, since there is motion.

Contestation, on the other hand, is opposed to non-contestation, for it is the elimination of that which is apparent by the positing of the non-evident thing—for example, the Stoic says that void does not exist, being non-evident; but once this denial is put forward, then that which is evident, namely motion, ought to be co-eliminated with it.  For if void does not exist, then motion does not occur either, according to the method already demonstrated.

Non-attestation, likewise, is opposed to attestation, for it is confirmation through self-evidence of the fact that the object of opinion is not such as it was believed to be—for example, if someone is approaching from far off, we conjecture, owing to the distance, that he is Plato.  But when the distance is reduced, we recognize through self-evidence that it is not Plato.  This sort of thing turns out to be non-attestation.

So attestation and non-contestation are the criterion of something’s being true, while non-attestation and contestation are the criterion of its being false.  And self-evidence is the foundation and basis of all [four] of these.” - Sextus Empiricus, Against the Professors 7.211-216

The principles of the Canon square up the cornerstones of empirical reasoning (click image at right to view diagram).  We need not dwell upon method of direct observation—its obvious application can be summarized by a single well-known maxim: “seeing is believing.”  But when confronted by inconspicuous phenomena, we must resort to the method of inference—its application is less obvious and deserves further explanation.

Methods of Inference

Our principal source for elucidation is Philodemus of Gadara, an Epicurean philosopher and poet who resided in Italy after about 80 BC.  A number of his writings have been found among the ruins of Herculaneum, an ancient resort town buried along with sister city Pompeii by the 79 AD eruption of Mt. Vesuvius.  One of the better preserved papyrus scrolls, designated PHerc. 1056, has been assigned the title: “On Signs,” or alternatively, “On Methods of Inference.”  In this polemical treatise, we find Philodemus defending the Epicurean method of analogy—the procedure by which we may infer the qualities of things which happen to lie outside the range of perception.

In accordance with Philodemus’ doctrine, we may say that an incontestable analogy has the following constituents:

1.       An agent, which is something inconspicuous.

2.       An attribute, which is a quality that we suppose exists in the agent.

3.       An analog, which is something conspicuous that obviously embodies the attribute.

4.       A sign, which is an obvious effect that is particular to the presence of the attribute.

5.       A hypothesized exception, which, by not occurring, affirms the particularity of the sign.

The analogy may be succinctly verbalized in the following format:

[The agent], insofar as [it contains the attribute], [exhibits the particular sign] because no known [analog] [exhibits the exception].

The ancient Epicureans put the method of analogy to heavy use.  Some classic examples include arguments for the solidity of the atoms, the intangibility of the void, the mortality of men, and the porous nature of the human skin.  Hence: atoms, insofar as they are bodies, are tangible, because no known body is intangible; the void, insofar as it is empty, allows motion, because no known empty place inhibits motion; all men, insofar as they are men, are mortal, because no known man has ever survived the thorough treatment of an executioner; the human skin, insofar as it is porous, allows the passage of sweat, because no known sieve inhibits flow of water.  These are examples of what Sextus Empiricus describes as non-contestation.

The linchpin of the argument by analogy hinges upon the particular relationship between the sign and the attribute—they must be inseparable.  If not, then the analogy doesn’t hold.  Such a case is said to be using a “common sign” rather than a “particular” one.  For instance, it would be unwise to argue as follows: atoms, insofar as they are bodies, are colored, because no known body is colorless; the void, insofar as it is intangible, in non-existent, because no known existing thing is intangible; all men, insofar as they are rich, are good, because no known rich man is evil.  These arguments fail because each uses a common sign, made evident by an exceptional case: a body immersed in darkness does not produce color; a man who acquired his wealth by theft is evil.  Any assertion to the contrary would be subject to what Sextus Empiricus describes as contestation—the elimination of something apparent.

Whether or not a particular analogy is valid, the method itself can always be rendered cogent—or in other words: the exception proves the rule.  A common sign may be just as useful in exposing a falsehood as a particular sign is for revealing a truth.  Hence, an analogy may be stated in an alternative format—the form of a denial:

[The agent] insofar as [it contains the attribute], [does not necessarily exhibit the sign] because [at least one member within the class of analogs] indeed [exhibits the exception].

Hence: atoms, insofar as they are bodies, are not necessarily colored, because some bodies are indeed colorless; all wealthy men, insofar as they are wealthy, are not necessarily good, because there are some rich men who are indeed evil.

Dogmatism?

Epicureanism has occasionally been labeled as dogmatism, or sometimes dogmatic empiricism, because Epicurus had resolved cosmological issues to a narrow position.  But he was justified in concluding that the universe is not so constituted that anything can happen anytime anywhere.  Nature has a nature, after all, and the ancient Epicureans argued that what we don’t see happen in the universe can be as informative as what we do see.  However, the Epicureans were also quite explicit in recognizing that some phenomena are riddled with ambiguities.

Fuzzy Logic

If you have ever come across the term fuzzy logic, chances are that you saw or heard it used in connection with computer science or the latest in electronic gadgetry.  Coined in 1964 by Azerbaijani-born American logician Lofti Zadeh, the application of fuzzy-logic has been sudden and far-reaching: thermostats that regulate temperature more efficiency, braking systems that stop vehicles more smoothly, camcorders that track a steady frame in spite of a jittering operator, optical character readers that more accurately convert images of printed pages into electronic text, computer games in which automated opponents play more competitively, and investing strategies that allow program-traders to play the markets more profitably.  But although this “fuzzy revolution” is recent and technological, the ideas behind it are ancient and philosophical.

Logic, as a formal discipline of deductive reasoning, is a product of the Hellenistic world.  Its name is derived from the Greek logos, meaning “word,” and its inventor is traditionally—and perhaps rightly—regarded as Aristotle.  In this system, every entity in the universe is presumed to be a member of a distinct category.  For instance, if Socrates is a man, he belongs to the category of all men.  Categories can also be subdivided into smaller categories (if Socrates is bald, he belongs to the subset of bald men) and subsumed by larger ones (all men are people—so Socrates belongs to the superset of all people).

Given the establishment of categories, Aristotle identified two axioms upon which the whole of his system rests: The Law of Non-Contradiction and the Law of the Excluded Middle.  The first of these can be expressed by the formula: “A cannot be both B and not-B (at the same time and in the same respect).”  E.g., “Socrates cannot be both a man and not a man.”  The second law, which is a corollary of the first, purports that “A is either B or not-B.”  E.g., “Socrates is either a man or not a man.”  Hence, the structure of this logical system is bivalent, meaning that every aspect of existence can be cleaved down to two ultimate possibilities: true or false, yes or no, is or isn’t, one or zero.

Fuzzy Logic Applied to Opinions

Returning now to the subject of opinions, must they be either true or not-true, or can they be more or less true?  In their 1993 book, Fuzzy Logic, authors Daniel McNeill and Paul Freiberger report:

In the Metaphysics, a few pages before announcing the Law of the Excluded Middle, [Aristotle] says, “The more and the less are still present in the nature of things” and adds that one who thinks 4 equals 5 is more correct than the one who thinks 4 equals, 1,000.  In De Interpretatione, he uses the terms truer and falser.  Even for him, truth has degrees.

In fact, Aristotle provides a wealth of arguments against his own axioms.  The most famous involves ships clashing at sea.  In Chapter 9 of De Interpretatione, he ponders the statement: 

“There will be a sea battle tomorrow.”

True?  Not True?

A determinist would argue that, at this instant, it must be either true or false.  Events are preordained, by divine hand or physical law.  If we knew enough about celestial will or the sequence of causes, we could tell right now whether it were true or false.

A non-determinist would demur.  Events are not preordained, so at this moment it may be impossible for us to know the answer, even with total knowledge.  ...  The answer may simply not exist yet.  If so, the statement is currently neither true nor false, but indeterminate.

De Interpretatione is a dense, muddled work, and the crucial Chapter 9 is so opaque that scholars still contest its meaning.  However, it appears to set forth these arguments, half-heartedly reject determinism, and conclude that “There will be a sea battle tomorrow” has an intermediate truth-value.  A gap opens at the hairline.  The Law of the excluded Middle ebbs away. - McNeill & Freiberger, Fuzzy Logic (1993), page 55

Epicurus however, a confirmed indeterminist, took a bold stand.  When confronted by a similar proposition, such as “Hermarchus will die tomorrow,” he flat out denied that a definite answer could be given.  For this reason, the ancient Epicureans caught a lot of flak from their contemporary rivals, the Stoics, who believed that truth and falsehood have a permanent existence.  Cicero, the renowned Roman orator, specifically writes:

…when you have two contradictories, and by contradictories here I mean a pair of statements where one asserts something while the other denies it—it is necessary, though Epicurus disagrees, that one be true, the other false, e.g., “Philoctetes will be wounded” was true in all previous ages, and “Philoctetes will not be wounded” false.  Unless perhaps we’re inclined to follow the opinion of the Epicureans, who say that such propositions are neither true nor false, or, when that shames them, they say something even more outrageous, namely that disjunctions of contradictories are true, but that of the original propositions contained in them, neither is true. - Cicero, On Fate 37

In the latter twentieth century, we see history repeating itself.  The modern proponents of the “included middle” have eschewed bivalence in favor multivalence—and they too have their share of outraged, outspoken detractors.  But multivalent logic is all about common sense: it is the addition of indeterminate between true and false, the addition of maybe between yes and no, the addition of somewhat between is and isn’t, the addition of fractions between one and zero.

This is how, to use Cicero’s terminology, “disjunctions can be true” (X+Y=1) when “neither proposition within them is true” (X¹1, Y¹1): indeterminate contradictories may add up to the truth (½+½=1), just as fixed ones do (0+1=1).

Coping with Ambiguity

The application of multivalence in the pursuit of truth crops up often in the corpus of extant Epicurean manuscripts.  In his Letter to Pythocles, for instance, Epicurus prefaces his account of celestial phenomena by stating that: 

It is not good to desire what is impossible, and to endeavor to enunciate a uniform theory about everything which we have followed in our researches into ethics, or in the solution of problems of natural philosophy.  … We cannot act in the same way with respect to the heavenly phenomena, these productions may depend upon several different causes, and we may give many different explanations on this subject, equally agreeing with the impression of the senses.  Besides, it is not here a question about reasoning on new principles, and of laying down, à priori, rules for the interpretation of nature; the only guides for us to follow are the appearances themselves. - Epicurus, Letter to Pythocles 85

Epicurus goes on to postulate, for example, that the moon might be luminous, or illuminated by the sun; eclipses of the sun and moon may be due to self-induced lulls, or the juxtaposition of interposing bodies; the appearance of a “face” on the moon may be due to incidental surface features, or the interposition of an atmospheric disturbance; comets may appear and disappear because their constituent particles condense and dissipate at random, or perhaps because they move as a whole between places nearer and further from us; meteors may be detached pieces of stars, or they may be substances which are “set on fire by the action of the wind.”  Epicurus also reminds Pythocles of the possibility that such causes may exist in tandem to bring about the phenomenon.  As it turns out, a modern astronomer would indeed testify that meteors are substances that do burn up on the atmosphere—lumps of iron which, moreover, did originate as chunks of exploding stars.

For Epicurus, then, any hypothesis is admissible as long as it meets the aforementioned criterion of non-contestation.  Although each hypothesis is not regarded as being wholly true or false when considered individually, they may add up to the truth when considered collectively.  By this means, the ancients could settle their minds on matters for which precise observation was not available, and still retain accuracy.

Lucretius, in writing his poem on nature, followed Epicurus’ lead, in providing multiple explanations not only for the phenomena of the sky, but also for earthly curiosities such as why the Nile floods in the summer, what gives magnets their attractive power, and the possible causes of diseases.  To be fair though, Epicurus and Lucretius certainly did not always hit their mark, even with their “shotgun approach.”  For instance, they attributed the westward migration of heavenly bodies either to their motion through space, or because they were fixed upon a rotating firmament encasing the earth—but never because the stars were fixed in space surrounding a rotating earth; earthquakes were attributed to wind penetrating the earth, or to the absorption of external matter from outer space—but never to grinding of the Earth’s migrating crust.  But perhaps the most infamous oversight among ancient Epicureans was their assumption that the apparent sizes of celestial bodies do not diminish over distance, when in fact they do—albeit imperceptibly at terrestrial distances.  In presuming that celestial imagery traveled in a laser-like parallel fashion, they concluded that the sun, moon, and stars were “more or less the same size as they appear to be.”   This attitude also brought upon the derisive criticism of Cicero—and we too might laugh loudly with him when he interprets this statement to mean that the sun must be just a foot across!  (though we don't know of any Epicurean having gone on record saying so.)  But take careful note of the nature of these errors: lack of contestation.  Not having a way to get much closer to the sky, or to probe the depths of the earth, what possible basis was there to contest the ancient Epicureans?  It took the invention of the telescope and the seismograph in order to reveal the surprising solutions we learn in our youth today.
